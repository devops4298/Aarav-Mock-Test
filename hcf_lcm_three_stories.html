The rule, once

Chapter 01 · Kitchen

The Cookie Jar Mystery

Counting · Sharing · Tidy Rows

Grandma bakes cookies every weekend for her two grandkids, Maya and Arjun. She has two rules.

One. Every cookie in Maya's box must be the same kind, and every cookie in Arjun's box must be the same kind — but Maya and Arjun can get different kinds.

Two. She wants the boxes to look tidy — same number of cookies in each row.

"The largest row size that works for both boxes is 6 cookies. And if I lined up all of Maya's cookies in one straight row, and all of Arjun's in another, the smallest length where the two rows would end at the same spot is 72 cookies."

She also lets slip: Maya has 24 cookies. How many cookies are in Arjun's box?

Two boxes, one shared rule. Watch the rows fall into place.

The reasoning, deliberately.
Open each step in order. Try to think one move ahead before you tap.

Strip the story of its decoration. Grandma said two things:

"The largest row that works for both" — that is simply the biggest number that divides both cookie counts evenly. Mathematicians call this the HCF (Highest Common Factor).

"The smallest row length where both lines would end together" — that is the smallest number both cookie counts go into evenly. That's the LCM (Lowest Common Multiple).

So the story is dressed-up math. Underneath, we know: HCF = 6, LCM = 72, one number = 24, find the other.

Here is a fact a kid can verify with any two numbers:

When you multiply two whole numbers, you get the same answer as multiplying their HCF and LCM.

a × b  =  HCF × LCM

Try it with 4 and 6 → HCF = 2, LCM = 12 → 2 × 12 = 24, and 4 × 6 = 24. ✓
Try 10 and 15 → HCF = 5, LCM = 30 → 5 × 30 = 150, and 10 × 15 = 150. ✓

It always works. The HCF is the "shared part" and the LCM is the "combined whole" — multiplied together, they perfectly account for both numbers.

Let Arjun's cookies = x. Then:

24 × x  =  6 × 72
24 × x  =  432
x  =  432 ÷ 24  =  18

No algebra needed beyond one division.

Check: does HCF(24, 18) really equal 6? Both 24 and 18 are divisible by 6 → 24 = 6 × 4, 18 = 6 × 3. No bigger number divides both. ✓

Check: does LCM(24, 18) really equal 72? 72 ÷ 24 = 3, 72 ÷ 18 = 4. Both divide it. No smaller number does. ✓

The story holds.

The answer
Arjun has 18 cookies.
Maya's 24 cookies tidy into 4 rows of 6. Arjun's 18 cookies tidy into 3 rows of 6. Same row size — Grandma's first rule. And 72 is the smallest length the two single-row lineups would both finish at.
Try at home

Pour out 24 buttons and 18 buttons on the table. Ask the kid: "What's the biggest equal group we can make in both piles, with nothing left over?" Watch them discover 6 with their own hands.

Chapter 02 · Schoolyard

The Track Sync

Motion · Timing · Meeting Points

Riya and Kabir run laps around the school's oval track every evening. They start side-by-side at the white line.

Riya runs a lap in 4 minutes. Kabir runs his at some other steady pace — slower than Riya. Their coach has been watching with a stopwatch, and she tells them two things at the end of the week.

"The longest stretch of time that fits cleanly into both your lap times is 2 minutes. And the first time you both finished a lap at exactly the same instant — after the start — was 20 minutes in."

Riya looks puzzled. Kabir starts thinking. How long does Kabir take per lap?

Two runners. Two paces. One quiet rule deciding when they meet.
HCF
2 min
LCM
20 min
Riya
4 min
Kabir
?
The reasoning, deliberately.
A track problem is a clock problem in disguise.

For both runners to be at the start line at the same instant, that moment in time has to be a whole number of Riya's laps and a whole number of Kabir's laps. The smallest such moment is, by definition, the LCM of their lap times.

The coach said that first joint moment was 20 minutes. So LCM = 20.

"The longest stretch that fits cleanly into both lap times" is asking: what's the biggest unit of time that divides both Riya's 4 minutes and Kabir's unknown lap time without a remainder?

That's exactly the HCF. The coach said 2 minutes. So HCF = 2.

Riya × Kabir  =  HCF × LCM
4 × Kabir  =  2 × 20
4 × Kabir  =  40
Kabir  =  10 minutes

So Kabir runs a lap every 10 minutes.

In 20 minutes, Riya runs 20 ÷ 4 = 5 laps. Kabir runs 20 ÷ 10 = 2 laps. Both whole numbers — so both are at the start line. ✓

And before 20 minutes? At 4, 8, 12, 16 minutes only Riya is at the line. At 10 minutes only Kabir is. They never both land there until 20. ✓

Hit "Jump to first meeting" on the animation above and watch it happen.

The answer
Kabir's lap takes 10 minutes.
In 20 minutes Riya completes 5 laps, Kabir completes 2. Both whole numbers — both at the line. The shared 2-minute "heartbeat" divides both 4 and 10. The rule held.
Try at home

Set two timers — one beeping every 4 seconds, one every 10. Listen for the first moment they beep together. Twenty seconds. The track problem, in a kitchen.

Chapter 03 · Festival Night

The Diwali Lights

Rhythm · Patterns · Sky-bright Moments

Anaya is decorating her balcony for Diwali. She strings up two long ropes of lights along the railing — one fitted with scarlet bulbs, the other with gold.

The scarlet string is set to a known rhythm: it blinks every 8 seconds. The gold string was a gift from her uncle, and she's lost the manual. She has no idea how often it blinks.

Sitting on the floor with her little brother, watching the lights against the night, she notices two patterns:

"The whole balcony lights up together every 24 seconds. And the smallest steady tick we can clap to — that fits both rhythms — is 4 seconds."

Her brother frowns. "So how often does the gold one blink?" What should Anaya tell him?

Watch for the moment both colors flare together. That's the LCM saying hello.
HCF
4 sec
LCM
24 sec
Scarlet
8 sec
Gold

The reasoning, deliberately.
A festival is just a clock dressed up in colour.

"The whole balcony lights up together every 24 seconds" — both strings have completed a whole number of their own cycles. That's the smallest time both blink intervals divide into evenly. LCM = 24.

"The smallest steady tick we can clap to that fits both rhythms" — the largest unit of time that divides both blink intervals. HCF = 4.

And we know scarlet = 8 seconds. We want gold.

The rule has not changed. It will never change. Two numbers multiplied equal their HCF times their LCM.

Scarlet × Gold  =  HCF × LCM
8 × Gold  =  4 × 24
8 × Gold  =  96
Gold  =  12 seconds

HCF(8, 12): both divisible by 4, no bigger number divides both. ✓
LCM(8, 12): smallest number divisible by both → 24. ✓

Pull up the canvas above. The first moment both bulbs flare together is at second 24. Before that — at 8, 16 (scarlet alone) and 12 (gold alone) — only one is lit.

The HCF of 4 seconds is the rhythm both strings secretly share, even though only the scarlet one blinks on 4-second beats and only on every second one of those. The gold blinks on every third 4-second beat. The HCF is the metronome neither string can deny.

This is why HCFs show up in music, in machinery, in heart-rates, in tides — wherever two periodic things share a planet.

The answer
The gold string blinks every 12 seconds.
Scarlet flashes at 8, 16, 24, 32… Gold flashes at 12, 24, 36… They overlap, for the first time, at second 24. And both can be felt on a 4-second tick — the shared heartbeat.
Try at home

Next time the kid sees two indicator lights blinking — on a router, a charger, a dashboard — ask: "When do you think they'll blink together?" Then time it. You're not doing math anymore. You're noticing it.
